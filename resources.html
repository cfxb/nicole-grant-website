Resources

These resources are for anyone dealing with anxiety, phobias (including emetophobia), or OCD — or for a parent trying to understand what their child is going through. They can help you make sense of what you're seeing and find support in BC. They are not a replacement for professional help, and nothing here is medical advice.

If you or someone you care about is in crisis or at risk right now, this is the part that matters most. In BC you can call or text 9-8-8 (Suicide Crisis Helpline), or call the BC Mental Health Support Line at 310-6789, any time. For a victim of crime, VictimLink BC is 1-800-563-0808. In an emergency, call 911.

Emetophobia (fear of vomiting)

What it is

Emetophobia is a strong fear of vomiting — of being sick yourself, seeing someone else be sick, or even just feeling nauseous. It's a type of phobia, and it's more common than most people realise. The fear is usually about what might happen, which makes it hard to reason your way out of it. That's not a weakness — it's just how this kind of fear works.

How to recognise it

In children. A child may complain of stomach aches, not want to eat, or refuse certain foods. They might avoid school if a classmate has been unwell, or worry a lot about getting sick on a car trip or plane. They may keep asking for reassurance: "Am I going to be sick? Are you sure?" Younger children often can't name what they're afraid of. It can just look like tummy complaints or distress that seems bigger than the situation warrants.

In adults. Adults often know the fear doesn't quite make sense, but that doesn't make it easier. It can show up as avoiding planes, restaurants, hospitals, or being around anyone who might be unwell. Some people check food dates carefully, or pay close attention to every feeling in their body. It can overlap with health anxiety or OCD.

When it may be okay to manage it yourself

If the fear is mild, hasn't been going on long, and isn't really getting in the way of daily life, good information and some general anxiety strategies may be enough to start. The links at the bottom of this section are a good place to begin. For parents: staying calm and matter-of-fact, without reorganising family life around the fear, can help a child feel safer.

When it may be worth seeing a counsellor

It's worth reaching out if the fear is getting in the way of ordinary life — school, work, eating, travel, or seeing friends. If you or your child is seeking reassurance every day, or feeling distressed most of the time, support can really help. Phobias respond well to gentle, gradual work. They rarely fade on their own.

When other care may be needed

If there's been significant weight loss, or eating and drinking are becoming restricted, please see a GP first. If the fear comes with low mood, panic attacks, severe OCD, or any thoughts of self-harm, a GP or psychiatrist can help figure out whether medication or other medical support should be part of the picture. Those decisions belong with a doctor.

Phobias more generally

What it is

A phobia is a strong, lasting fear of something specific — dogs, heights, needles, flying, enclosed spaces, and many others. The fear shows up reliably whenever the thing is nearby or expected, and most people cope by avoiding it. That avoidance tends to make the fear stronger over time. Phobias are very common, and they often start in childhood.

How to recognise it

In children. A child may become very upset, freeze, cry, cling, or have a meltdown when faced with the feared thing. Reassurance and explanations usually don't help much — the fear isn't something they can simply switch off. Younger children may not see the fear as unreasonable. It can get in the way of school, friendships, and family life.

In adults. Adults usually know the fear is out of proportion, but still feel intense anxiety when they face it — racing heart, sweating, shaking, or a strong urge to get away. The main sign is avoidance, and the way that avoidance quietly narrows what feels possible in daily life.

When it may be okay to manage it yourself

If a fear is mild and isn't really limiting life, starting with good information and gentle self-guided steps may be enough. The links below are a solid starting point. For a child, calm encouragement to face the feared thing in small steps — without pushing or forcing — can make a real difference.

When it may be worth seeing a counsellor

It's worth getting support when the phobia is getting in the way of everyday life, when you find yourself avoiding more and more, or when self-help hasn't made a dent. Phobias generally respond well to structured, step-by-step work done at a pace that feels manageable to you.

When other care may be needed

If the phobia comes with frequent panic attacks, low mood, another anxiety problem, or any thoughts of self-harm, it's worth talking to a GP or psychiatrist. They can help decide whether medication or other medical care should be part of the plan. That's a decision to make with a doctor, not from a website.
